Lampshades

The Genesis

This project’s genesis lies in my growing interest in the CAD software Rhino and its add-on Grasshopper. At the time of this project, Summer 2022, I was working at Fluxspace as an intern in their makerspace, and had recently learned of the existence of Grasshopper through a brief chat with Adam and Alex at Dive Design. After a week-long YouTube bender of different tutorials and projects, I felt that I had the confidence in the software to create objects that I found online or in the world around me. One of the very first tutorials that most Grasshopper guides take you through is a vase or lampshade (which are secretly the same thing). These tutorials inspired me to branch off on my own and create my own lamps and my own tutorials. I don’t have many visuals from this project, so here's a link to an Instagram reel by Artillery 3D—the folks whose printer created the piece.

The Mechanics

Speaking as to how I designed this is slightly mechanically intensive; I’ll get into the nitty gritty here. I started by creating a dodecahedron in Grasshopper. This was harder than I initially anticipated, because I needed to locate the center point of each face in 3D space. I made this easier on myself by using cylindrical coordinates. After manually calculating the positions for these faces, I used the polygon tool to create a properly oriented pentagon skew from the base. Then, I used a circular pattern to create the other 4 faces, leaving me with the bottom half of a dodecahedron. From here, I simply mirrored the bottom half about the center point of the full dodecahedron (whose location I also had to manually calculate) and rotated it to fit properly. After constructing my dodecahedron, I needed to actually create the stellation. Simply put, a stellation pattern is a point extrusion about each face. With this in mind, I used the area tool to find the centroid of each face and created SDL (start, direction, length) lines to each centroid. I started each line at the center of the dodecahedron, directing them towards the centroids of the face, and finally set the length to a slider to allow users to adjust how pointy they want the piece to be. In my case, I chose “very pointy.”

Education Tie-In

A good while after this project, I found myself wanting to share the joys of procedural CAD with others. I partnered with my friend Drew to create a workshop all about the construction of lampshades. I told some friends from another college about the workshop and they requested that I provide a remote option. While it's not a guide on how to create the lampshade from this post, the video here takes users through everything that I love about Grasshopper and how to make your own lampshade.
