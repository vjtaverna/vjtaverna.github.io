nTop Lattice Construction: DfAM

A New Horizon

Anyone who has worked with me for any portion of time will immediately realize my obsession with design for additive manufacturing (DfAM). Over the Summer, I was introduced to a new CAD software (and design methodology) called nTop. nTop is a software that integrates algorithmic CAD with intense math. This lets users define cells maps and patterns to construct complex patterns with a few simple steps. You may think, "Well that piece looks cool, but what's the point of that? Won't the added complexity increase the cost?" This is a wise thought in a conventional manufacturing setting, but the advantage of additive manufacturing is that complexity is free. Considering that, we can design pieces with variable density to only add reinforcement to the locations that need it.

Mechanical Engineering Beauty

If you're anything like me at this point, you're starting to get really excited. Let's consider a mechanical engineer's dream: a cantilevered beam in bending. When we model this, we find the maximum bending moment at the joint with the wall. If all we concern ourselves with is beam breakage and not deformation, then making the beam uniform thickness all the way through is a waste of material. The beam should only be as thick as the maximum bending moment requires it to be. Latticing allows users to decrease the strength (and therefore weight and material use) in places that have been overdesigned without interrupting the overall shape of the piece. In the case of a simply supported beam, we can easily just taper the beam to reduce material cost. However, this extra manufacturing cost can defeat the purpose of redesigning the part in the first place. Additive manufacturing empowers users to recreate their part without any added costs.

Artful Beauty

In addition to these beautiful engineering applications, this software can also be used to make some pretty asthetic pieces. In my visual thinking class, I adapted this software to create a reaction piece to Vincent Fecteau's work in the Carnegie Museum of Art.
