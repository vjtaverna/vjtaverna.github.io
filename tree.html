Parametrizing Design of 3D Printed Trees

The Seed

In my exploration of Grasshopper, I developed a skillset that I was eager to unleash on new projects. As such, when my contacts at Dive Design reached out about a project that would let me use these skills, I jumped at the opportunity. In short, Adam asked me to recreate the trees from their sister company Willo, using Grasshopper. This is the amazing part about design for additive manufacturing (DfAM)—we can create truly custom parts and allow consumers to truly take control of the products they receive. Using ShapeDiver, a service that allows designers to host their Grasshopper scripts online, we can give consumers the opportunity to vary the input parameters of the design to create their own custom tree. In addition to this, ShapeDiver offers AR compatibility to visualize how their version of their product would look in their space. This project speaks to the exact vision I dream of when using Grasshopper—democratizing CAD.

More on Mechanics

As for the meat and potatoes of how this works, the piece starts with a cylindrical coordinate system to graph a curve (shown here in green). Afterwards, I take the curve and create a polar array, by varying the number of copies of this curve, I can change the cross sectional shape as well. Next, I take these curves and non-uniformly scale them down in the radial direction so that I can use a block called “weave” that alternates between selecting from two lists. Using this new weaved list, I can loft between them to create a surface. There’s a little bit of wizardry that goes on behind the scenes to make it work Just Right, but that’s beyond the scope of this post. After that, I generate the branches by starting points in the trunk and drawing lines in a direction slightly elevated from the horizon. I use the same method with a reoriented plane inside the branch to draw smaller branches. For the leaves, I used a deformed cylindrical plate that I just copied to the end of each branch.
